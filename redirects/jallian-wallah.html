JallianWala Bagh Massacre

1. Background & Build-Up After World War I, India was facing political unrest. The British had promised political reforms in return for Indian support in the war, but instead passed the Rowlatt Act (1919), which allowed: • Arrest without warrant • Imprisonment without trial • Trials without jury This was seen as a betrayal, and protests erupted all over the country. In Punjab, the protests were especially strong. Two popular leaders, Dr. Saifuddin Kitchlew and Dr. Satyapal, were arrested on 10 April 1919 in Amritsar for opposing the Rowlatt Act. This sparked anger among the people.

2. The Gathering at Jallianwala Bagh • Date: 13 April 1919 (Baisakhi day — an important Punjabi festival) • Place: Jallianwala Bagh, Amritsar — an enclosed garden surrounded by high walls, with only a few narrow exits. • Why People Came: 1.Many villagers came to Amritsar for the Baisakhi fair. 2.Others came to attend a peaceful meeting to protest against the Rowlatt Act and demand the release of Kitchlew and Satyapal. Important: The meeting was peaceful and unarmed.

3. The Massacre • The British military officer General Reginald Dyer heard about the gathering and considered it a violation of his ban on public meetings. • Without any warning to disperse, Dyer: 1.Took about 50 riflemen. 2.Blocked the main exit. 3.Ordered firing directly into the crowd. • Duration of firing: Around 10 minutes. • Bullets fired: Approximately 1,650 rounds. • Casualties (official British figures): 379 killed, over 1,200 injured. Indian sources claimed the numbers were much higher.

4. Immediate Impact • Panic and chaos — many died from stampede, bullet wounds, or jumping into the well inside the Bagh to escape. • News spread rapidly, shocking the entire nation. • The British government praised Dyer in Britain initially, but Indian outrage was massive.

5. National Reaction • Rabindranath Tagore returned his knighthood in protest. • Gandhi called for non-cooperation with the British — this incident became a turning point in his leadership of the freedom movement. • The Hunter Commission was set up by the British to investigate. While it criticized Dyer, no real punishment was given, increasing Indian anger.

6. Historical Significance • Exposed the brutality of British rule to the whole world. • Destroyed the myth that the British ruled with the consent of Indians. • Inspired thousands to join the freedom struggle.• • Became one of the key reasons for launching the Non-Cooperation Movement (1920).
